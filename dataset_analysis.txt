Dataset Analysis: Synthetic vs. Real-World SDN Traffic
1. The Synthetic Dataset (Our "Trend-Based" Data)
We engineered a specific "Proactive Load Prediction" scenario to test the limits of static vs. temporal models. Here is the breakdown of its logic:

A. Core Pattern: Deterministic Trends
Baseline: A sine wave (base_load = 50 + 30 * sin(t)) simulates daily traffic cycles.
The "Attack" Rule: We hard-coded a specific Trend-Based Anomaly:
If traffic rises for 3 consecutive steps (t-2 < t-1 < t), then the NEXT step (t+1) will spike by 300%.

Why we did this: This creates a Hidden Temporal Dependency.
A Static Model (Random Forest) at time t sees only the current value. It doesn't know how it got there (rising or falling). It sees Load=50 and predicts Normal.
A Temporal Model (LSTM) sees the sequence [30, 40, 50]. It recognizes the "Rising Trend" pattern and predicts Overload for t+1.
B. The Labeling Strategy (Proactive)
We labeled the data based on Future Events (t+1, t+2), not current state.

Normal (0): Future load is safe.
Medium (1): Future load > 50%.
Overload (2): Future load > 80% OR a spike occurs.
2. Real-World SDN Datasets (e.g., Mininet / Real ISP Logs)
Real-world data is significantly messier and less deterministic.

A. Characteristics
Stochastic Traffic: Trends don't follow perfect rules. User behavior is random (Poisson processes), not sinusoidal. Bursts happen due to external events (news, flash crowds), not just "3 steps of rise".
Noise & Jitter: Real sensors have measurement error. Packet counts fluctuate wildly due to network jitter, even when load is constant.
Non-Stationarity: The "patterns" act differently on Monday morning vs. Sunday night. A "rising trend" on Sunday might just be a backup job, while on Monday it's a DDoS attack.
High Dimensionality: Real controllers (ONOS, OpenDaylight) export 100+ metrics (Port stats, Flow table miss rates, CPU per core, etc.), not just the 5 we simulated.
B. "The Gap"
Synthetic: Clean, Perfect Rules. If you learn the rule, you get 100% accuracy (theoretically).
Real-World: No perfect rules exist. The maximum theoretical accuracy might only be 85% because some bursts are truly random and unpredictable.
3. What to Expect on Real-World Data?
If you deploy our Hybrid Ensemble on real data (e.g., from a Mininet testbed or a public dataset like CIC-IDS2017), expect the following:

📉 Accuracy Drop
Synthetic Accuracy: ~75-86%
Real-World Accuracy: Likely 60-75% initially.
Reason: The real world has "Aleatoric Uncertainty" (randomness) that no model can predict.
🚀 Increased Value of the Hybrid Model
In the real world, the LSTM component becomes even more critical.
Static rules (Random Forest) fail harder because real traffic is never "static."
The LSTM's ability to learn "fuzzy" temporal patterns (e.g., "traffic usually rises for 10 minutes before a crash") is your main advantage over baseline models.
🛠️ Drift & Retraining
Real networks change. A model trained on Week 1 data might fail on Week 2.
Solution: You would need an "Online Learning" pipeline where the model retrains every night.
